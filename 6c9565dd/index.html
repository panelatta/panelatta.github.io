The Method of Lagrangian Multipliers and The KKT Conditions

This article simply introduced strategies for finding the stationary points of the objective function subject to one or more equality or inequality constraints.

Consider a standard form of continuous optimization problem,

min⁡xf(x)s.t.    gk(x)≤0,    k=1,2,⋯ ,mhl(x)=0,    l=1,2,⋯ ,p\min\limits_{\bf x} f({\bf x}) \\ {\rm s.t.}\;\;g_k({\bf x})\leq0,\;\;k=1,2,\cdots,m \\ h_l({\bf x})=0,\;\;l=1,2,\cdots,p \\ xmin​f(x)s.t.gk​(x)≤0,k=1,2,⋯,mhl​(x)=0,l=1,2,⋯,p

in which

x∈ℜnf,gk,hl:ℜn→ℜ    for    k=1,2,⋯ ,m    and    l=1,2,⋯ ,pm,p≥0{\bf x} \in \Re^{n} \\ f,g_k,h_l:\Re^n \rightarrow \Re\;\;{\rm for}\;\;k=1,2,\cdots,m\;\;{\rm and}\;\;l=1,2,\cdots,p \\ m,p\geq0 x∈ℜnf,gk​,hl​:ℜn→ℜfork=1,2,⋯,mandl=1,2,⋯,pm,p≥0

And f,gk,hlf,g_k,h_lf,gk​,hl​ are all continuous differentable.

We divided the problem into two cases: p=0p = 0p=0 or p≠0p \neq 0p​=0. For the former we introduced The Method of Lagrange Multipliers as the solving strategy, and simply introduced KKT Conditions for the other one when it suits some Regularity Conditions.

Notice that it was easy to treat a maximization problem by negating the objective function, we only use the maximization problem as a general example.

KKT Conditions

For convinience, we consider the case without equality constraint but with a single inequality constraint first,

min⁡xf(x)s.t.    g(x)≤0.\min_{\bf x}f({\bf x}) \\ {\rm s.t.} \;\; g({\bf x}) \leq 0. xmin​f(x)s.t.g(x)≤0.

Then define the feasible region K={ℜn ∣ g(x)≤0}\mathbb{K}=\left\{ \Re^n\,|\,g({\bf x}) \leq 0 \right\}K={ℜn∣g(x)≤0} and assuming that x∗{\bf x}^*x∗ is the best solution under the constraint condition ggg. According to whether x∗{\bf x}^*x∗ is on the border of K\mathbb{K}K or not, we can divide the problem into two cases and discuss them separately.

CASE  1.    g(x∗)<0.{\bf C{\scriptsize ASE}\; 1}.\;\; g({\bf x}^*) <0.CASE1.g(x∗)<0. The best solution is inside K\mathbb{K}K. At this time we call x∗{\bf x}^*x∗ as the interior solution. Obviously, at this time, an infinitesimal displacement of the point towards any direction will not against the constraint, so we call that the constraint conditionggg is inactive.

CASE  2.    g(x∗)=0.{\bf C{\scriptsize ASE}\;2}.\;\;g({\bf x}^*)=0.CASE2.g(x∗)=0. The best solution is on the border of K\mathbb{K}K. At this time we call x∗{\bf x}^*x∗ as the boundary solution. Correspondingly, now we call that the constraint condition ggg is active.

Likely, defining the Lagrangian Function

L(x,λ)=f(x)+λg(x)L({\bf x},\lambda)=f({\bf x})+\lambda g({\bf x}) L(x,λ)=f(x)+λg(x)

According to ggg is active or inactive, the necessary condition for us to get the best solution are different.

${\bf C{\scriptsize ASE};I{\scriptsize NACTIVE}}. $Cause the constraint condition ggg has no influence on getting the best solution, we could make λ=0\lambda = 0λ=0 directly. Now the task is equivalent to unconstrained optimization, and only ▽f=0\bigtriangledown f={\bf 0}▽f=0 is needed.

CASE  ACTIVE.{\bf C{\scriptsize ASE} \; A{\scriptsize CTIVE}}.CASEACTIVE. Now the constraint condition ggg is equivalent to

g(x)=0g({\bf x})=0 g(x)=0

Notice that for every points x∈g{\bf x} \in gx∈g, there is ▽g(x)\bigtriangledown g({\bf x})▽g(x) orthogonal to ggg. Likely, it is obvious to find that ▽f(x∗)\bigtriangledown f({\bf x}^*)▽f(x∗) is also orthogonal to ggg. So, we can easily prove that ▽f∈span(▽g)\bigtriangledown f \in {\rm span}(\bigtriangledown g)▽f∈span(▽g) at x∗{\bf x}^*x∗. That is to say, there exists λ\lambdaλ which makes that

▽xf=−λ▽xg\bigtriangledown_{\bf x} f=-\lambda \bigtriangledown_{\bf x} g ▽x​f=−λ▽x​g

It’s easy to find that λ≥0\lambda \geq 0λ≥0 should be kept, cause we want to minimize fff, and ▽f(x∗)\bigtriangledown f({\bf x}^*)▽f(x∗) (pointing to the fastest growing direction) should point to the interior of K\mathbb{K}K. However, ▽g\bigtriangledown g▽g points to the outside of K\mathbb{K}K, so λ\lambdaλ should be kept not less than000, which is called dual feasibility. Likely, if we want to maximize fff, we should keep λ≤0\lambda \leq 0λ≤0.

Obviously, there will always be either λ\lambdaλ or ggg equal to 000, so it always holds that λg(x)=0\lambda g({\bf x})=0λg(x)=0, which is called complementary slackness.
